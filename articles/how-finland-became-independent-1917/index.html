How Finland Became Independent (1917)

Finland’s independence in 1917 was not a sudden or accidental event. It was the result of long historical developments, political change, and a unique moment of opportunity created by upheaval in Europe. Understanding how Finland became an independent state helps explain its political culture, its institutions, and its strong emphasis on sovereignty and democracy.

Finland before independence

For centuries, Finland was not an independent country. From the Middle Ages until 1809, it formed the eastern part of the Kingdom of Sweden. During this period, Swedish laws, administration, and institutions shaped Finnish society. Swedish became the language of government and higher education, while Finnish was spoken mainly by the rural population.

In 1809, Sweden lost Finland to Russia after a war. Finland became an autonomous Grand Duchy within the Russian Empire. Although the Russian Emperor ruled Finland, the country retained its own laws, legal system, and administration. This autonomy allowed Finnish institutions and a sense of national identity to develop.

Did you know?

Finland kept its own currency, legal system, and Lutheran religion during Russian rule — a rare degree of autonomy within the Russian Empire.

The rise of Finnish national consciousness

During the 19th century, Finnish language and culture gained increasing importance. Writers, scholars, and political thinkers promoted Finnish as a national language and emphasized the idea that Finns formed a distinct people with their own history and traditions.

At the same time, political reforms expanded participation in public life. In 1906, Finland introduced a parliamentary system with universal suffrage, becoming one of the first countries in Europe to grant women the right to vote and stand for election.

Did you know?

Finnish women were among the first in the world to gain full political rights, including the right to be elected to parliament.

The impact of the Russian Revolution

Finland’s path to independence was directly influenced by events in Russia. In 1917, the Russian Empire collapsed during the Russian Revolution. The authority of the Russian Emperor disappeared, creating uncertainty about who held power over Finland.

Finnish political leaders argued that sovereignty should now belong to the Finnish people. With no clear central authority in Russia, Finland had a unique opportunity to define its own future.

The Declaration of Independence

On 6 December 1917, the Finnish Parliament approved a Declaration of Independence. The declaration stated that Finland was a sovereign state and no longer under Russian rule.

Independence was not automatically accepted by other countries. Finland needed international recognition to secure its position. In early 1918, the new Soviet government in Russia formally recognized Finland’s independence, followed by other states.

Did you know?

6 December is celebrated every year as Finland’s Independence Day.

Challenges after independence

Independence did not immediately bring stability. In 1918, Finland experienced a brief but violent civil war between opposing political groups. The conflict left deep social divisions but also reinforced the importance of democratic institutions and the rule of law in later decades.

Over time, Finland developed into a parliamentary democracy with strong legal protections, an independent judiciary, and a commitment to social cohesion.

Why this matters for future citizens

Finland’s independence explains many features of Finnish society today. The emphasis on democracy, legality, and national sovereignty is rooted in the experience of building a state under challenging conditions. Understanding this history helps future citizens appreciate the value placed on political participation, equality, and trust in institutions.
